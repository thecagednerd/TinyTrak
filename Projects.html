Project Origin

The project was originally bare bones, a box with a fingerprint reader and a network connection. This project had sat dormant for some time before I was asked how it could be improved and deployed to a customer.

Additional hardware and aesthetic touches

Aesthetically, this was just a beige box with a highly noticeable black protrusion near the lower point of the door. First task was to sink the fingerprint reader into the door for a lower profile.

This had been discussed before, though a clean way of lowering the profile was not found due to arc present in the design.

A 3D model was created and part was printed to neatly fill this space and cover the cut lines in the door of the enclosure.

PSU

A reclaimed alarm panel PSU was used in the initial build, this was unfortunately not powerful enough to run the additional hardware and was replaced with a Meanwell PSU that also boasted a battery backup.

Touch screen

In conjunction with a Raspberry Pi 4, a  Hannspree display was chosen to enrich features such as…

  • New starter/visitor induction and sign in
  • Local information, Hospitals, Police stations or shops and supermarkets.
  • Worker access to hours worked, available holidays, holiday booking, etc

Thermal receipt printer and camera

One of the three prototype units had a thermal receipt printer added, this was planned for visitor badge printing brasic details of hours and holidays that a worker may want to print out.

Solar frames

 A common requirement in this employment was to “Use what’s out there” ; this was very apparent when I discovered how engineers were attaching a  solar panel to a RedCop tower.

A door hinge was welded to a metal plate which was bolted to the mast, the other side of the hinge was bolted directly to the solar panel, there was no method of securing the lower end of the panel.

The reason for the damage on this tower is a falling tree during a storm, after this panel and plate was replaced, myself and the engineer found it difficult to lift the panel and gain access to the doors, a change of mounting method was due.

This is the first mounting system made for a single panel, much stronger angle brackets were used for mounting to the holes in the mast, the support leg on the lower end of the panel bolts into existing threaded holes in the chassis. This leg allowed clear access to the doors of the chassis.

Double up, using two panels

 Adoption of true MPPT solar controllers to replace the older PWM controllers meant that two panels could be used in parallel for a greater solar yield. A newer and more flexible system was needed. The aluminum extrusions were replaced with Unistrut, the length of the panel mounting rails and the leg made the frame almost universal, it would bolt to the RedCop style chassis and the V-ceptor chassis.

I trained the field engineers on how these frames were assembled and how they were to be packed and transported.Tthere was often a need for frames to be made from scratch on days where I may not have been available. A guide was written on the length of Unistrut required and the hardware and quantities needed. To the left is an example of an image used in a guide

Gaining Unistrut skills .I

After a short time I was fully comfortable with making solid Unistrut structures for holding panels and even self contained solar camera systems.

In the above example, solar was being added to an existing deployment, rotating the RedCop chassis south for a normal panel deployment was not possible, the lower end of the framework had verticals hammered into the ground.

Gaining Unistrut skills .II

 Part of a particularly challenging night in North Wales, myself and an engineer had to deploy 4 self contained solar CCTV units to two different sites for the same customer. In this small collection you will see the Unistrut framework developed on-the-spot to hold this unit securely in the ISO corner of a shipping container. The two diagonal lengths gave stability and rigidity in the X and Y dimensions, each diagonal had a horizontal partner  which ran along the top surface of the container and bolted in through the top ISO hole.

The Z was handled by the two parallel lengths secured through the side hole on the ISO mount.

Designing metalwork

The very first batch of V-ceptor chassis converted to the new system had the mast solar collar and antenna mounts recycled from partnered hardware that was no longer required. When brand new chassis were to arrive I designed metal work to match.

The left part is half an antenna mount, on the right is half a solar mast clamp. Each part is paired with an identical part, rotated 180 degrees. The reasons behind this were to reduce the number of unique parts in the frame, the other reason is for manufacturing, by having the same part twice Vs having a left and right pair, there is a better price break as 20x one part was being ordered not 10x left and 10x right, this also mitigates an engineer accidentally taking 2x left or right and not a pair.

The antenna mount has a slot to allow for an older type of antenna with a fixed cable, should the mount need to be completely removed or an assembly operative forget to pass the wire through, having to fully remove the entire length of cable would not be required. An added bonus to the slot Vs a hole is that during the laser cutting, the hole is not part of the perimeter cut and does not require the added step of having to stop the laser to make a hole, this small change dropped the part cost slightly.

Left: Two antenna mounts bolted together to form a complete mount, the black whip antenna on the left is the older style with fixed cable, mentioned above, the white antenna is for 4G mobile data.

.A number of neck brace parts had been lost or had failed, so I was tasked with designing replacements. The original design required a 50mm section of pipe to be cut into two halves and have two tabs with holes welded to one, and four tabs welded to the other.

My replacement design rotated the split line of the part 90 degrees, thi also allowed the part, as before the same part rotated 90 degrees not a left and right pair, to pull double duty and also replace the solar mast clamp. The wings eliminate two of the 90 degree brackets used on the solar frame.

The blue box section is part of the neck brace, this holds the mast at an angle during transit, this box section and/or the neck brace clamp has been left behind by sone engineers while  heading out to collect a tower, or it has been assumed that another engineer has left the parts inside the chassis. By the box section forming part of the clamp and the clamp also pulling double duty as part of the solar panel mounting system, it will always be with the tower.

Self made tool

On the old RedCop system, it was fairly common for an engineer to accidentally set up detectors to give false by them picking up road traffic or people outside the perimeter. I was showed how to connect to a webpage hosted on the RedCop to see its detections, while this was better than not knowing, it took a while to refresh and was very a very clunky interface over 3G.

Using a Masthead receiver board from a decommissioned tower, a RS232-USB converter and a USB OTG hub with my tablet, I was able to see detections down the second on my Android screen.

THis allowed me to provide a really crisp detection area with minimal chance of false alarms being sent through to the control room. The Masthead board ha a wide range of supply voltage and was happy enough to run off a 9V battery, only being used for a few minutes at a time, the battery wa more than enough for several site visits when I was required to go.  

PCB design

The first board I designed for this company was meant to replace a wooden board and some crimp terminals, there was a RUT955 router, a 24V-12V step-down to power the wired detectors and a small amplifier for a speaker.

The board was designed with a combination of calipers, 3D modeling and free online board editing software.

The Darkfighter head tower that this was designed for was phased out and none of these boards were ever spun up.

2XX series towers.

The very first batch of cameras were required so quickly that they went straight into build, looms and connections were made with existing parts, despite not looking the best they functioned as expected and over 30 more units were built with PCBs and were improved upon with each build.

This is a V1 control board, RS232 connectivity issue was resolved in the next revision.

Fingerboard

After the very first board of 2XX towers went out, I didn’t feel having time put into cutting, stripping and terminal drivers would be a good thing, there were plenty of 4 way 150mm cables ordered for connecting MPPT TTL to the control board, so to have ease of use at testing and assembly, the Fingerboard was made.

This scale paper cut out was used to check dimensions.

The actual board was screwed into the terminals to convert the Masthead board to a plug in cable.
